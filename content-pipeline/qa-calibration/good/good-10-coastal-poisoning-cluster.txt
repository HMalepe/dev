Four unrelated households in a fishing community near Saldanha Bay reported severe illness within the same week. One member of one household did not survive.

Deon Fortuin, sixty-seven, died two days after the onset of symptoms his family initially attributed to food poisoning from a communal fish braai, according to the family's statement recorded in the provincial health department's cluster investigation.

The mechanism, established by toxicology testimony cited in that investigation, was a marine biotoxin associated with a specific type of algal bloom, present at a concentration the report describes as "far in excess" of levels considered safe for consumption, found in tissue samples from the shellfish served at the braai.

The unraveling came from the health department's own monitoring data: according to the investigation report, water samples from the harvesting site had flagged elevated toxin levels five days before the braai, but the closure notice, per the department's internal correspondence entered into the report, was never issued to local harvesters due to what the report calls "an administrative delay."

The health department's findings are public record, and the matter is reportedly still under internal review, per the most recent departmental statement.
