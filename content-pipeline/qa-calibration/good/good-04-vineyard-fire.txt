A storage barn on a Stellenbosch-area wine estate burned down at 3am on a still, windless night. The estate's insurer flagged the claim before the ash had cooled.

The barn housed farm equipment and, according to the estate's own inventory records submitted to the insurance investigation, roughly two seasons' worth of unsold stock that had failed to move due to a distribution dispute earlier that year. The estate owner was not present on the property that night, per the security company's visitor log.

The mechanism the fire investigator's report focused on was point of origin: accelerant-pattern analysis, described in the report as consistent with a liquid accelerant poured along two separate points rather than one, which investigators noted is unusual for an accidental electrical fire, the estate's initial explanation.

What broke the case, according to the investigator's findings, was a fuel purchase: a jerry can bought in cash at a filling station eighteen kilometres away, four hours before the fire, captured on the station's own timestamped camera footage, and later matched by receipt to a vehicle registered to a former estate employee.

The former employee was charged with arson; the matter proceeded to trial, and court records show the case remains active.
