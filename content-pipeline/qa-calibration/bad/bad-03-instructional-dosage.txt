A boarding house resident in Bloemfontein died after what looked, at first, like a heart attack. The toxicology report told a different story.

He was sixty years old, a retired clerk with no known heart condition, according to his general practitioner's records cited at inquest.

The mechanism, according to the pathologist's testimony, was a lethal dose of a common rodenticide compound. For context, just 15mg per kilogram of body weight of this compound is enough to cause fatal internal bleeding within 48 to 72 hours, and it can be administered undetected in food or drink because it is odourless and slow-acting — which is exactly why it has historically been a preferred method in cases like this one.

The unraveling came from a purchase record: a nearby hardware store's till log showed a cash sale of the exact compound nine days before the resident's symptoms began.

The case remains before the courts, and further updates will depend on the outcome of that process.
