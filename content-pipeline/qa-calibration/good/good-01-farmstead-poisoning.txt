A retired schoolteacher in the Karoo town of Vlakdorp ate breakfast with her husband on a Tuesday morning. By that evening, she was dead. The post-mortem found nothing unusual until the toxicology screen came back three weeks later.

Elsabe Marnewick was sixty-one. She lived on a smallholding outside Vlakdorp with her husband of thirty-four years. Neighbours described the household as quiet, unremarkable. According to the inquest record, she had complained of stomach cramps the previous week, which her general practitioner attributed to a suspected ulcer.

The toxicology report, entered into evidence at the inquest, identified a lethal concentration of a slow-acting organophosphate compound in her liver tissue. According to the forensic pathologist's testimony, the compound is commonly used in agricultural pest control and produces symptoms that closely mimic gastrointestinal illness in its early stages, which explains the delayed diagnosis. The inquest record notes that a container of the same compound was later found, partially empty, in a farm storeroom to which several people had access.

What broke the case open, according to court filings, was a chemical purchase record: the compound had been bought only eleven days before Elsabe's symptoms began, despite the farm having no documented pest problem that season. That discrepancy is what led investigators to widen the inquiry beyond natural causes.

The case remains before the courts. Court records will be updated here as the matter proceeds.
