Three members of a small religious fellowship in a township outside Gqeberha died within the same month. Each death, on its own, had a plausible natural explanation.

According to the joint inquest that followed, all three were adult members of a fellowship led by a figure court documents refer to only as "the group's leader," pending separate legal proceedings. The fellowship met on a smallholding at the edge of the township, per property records cited in the inquest file.

The mechanism common to all three deaths, according to the forensic pathologist's combined testimony, was severe dehydration and electrolyte imbalance consistent with prolonged fasting under supervised conditions, a practice the inquest heard was part of the fellowship's documented rituals. Toxicology in each case, per the inquest record, ruled out common natural causes of sudden collapse.

The unraveling, according to court filings, came from a fellowship member who left the group and provided investigators with handwritten fasting schedules recovered from the property — schedules that, per the inquest transcript, matched the timeline of each death within a day.

Legal proceedings against the fellowship's leadership are ongoing, and court records will be the basis for any update to this case.
